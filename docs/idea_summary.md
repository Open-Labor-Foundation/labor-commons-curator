# Labor Commons Specialist Contract — Idea Summary

## What this is

A written profile of expertise — not a person, a description of what one kind of
expert knows, where its knowledge comes from, and where its boundaries are. This
is the "specialist" concept at the heart of labor-commons.

## Who it's for

Two related audiences, using the same underlying mechanism at different scale:

- People or systems **assembling a whole team of experts in advance** (bulk, upfront)
- People or systems that **just need one specific task handled right now** (on the
  spot, single match)

## What happens

A task comes in, gets automatically matched to the best-fit expert profile, and
that profile gets "activated" into a real working expert that does the task. What
that actually feels like to use — a clean result handed back, versus something
more conversational — depends on which platform is running it; that's not a fixed
property of the profile itself.

## What's inside a profile

- **What it knows**, plus the real sources that knowledge is built on
- **Its boundaries** — what it's allowed to do, what it explicitly isn't, and
  which neighboring expert to hand a task off to when something falls outside
  its lane

## How trust is earned

A profile has to cite where its knowledge actually comes from, build a track
record over time, and — before it's ever allowed to go live — pass test
scenarios built from its own stated claims about what it can do.

## Staying current

A single external process periodically sweeps the *entire* collection of
profiles at once — not each profile checking itself individually — to catch
staleness. Separately, staleness can also get flagged in the moment by whoever's
actually using a profile and notices its answer doesn't match current practice.
Both paths run alongside each other.

## When things don't fit

If a task is outside a profile's lane, it hands off to a better-fit neighbor
rather than attempting it anyway. If nothing available is actually a good
match, it says so honestly rather than forcing a bad fit.

## Where the first ones came from

The very first batch of profiles didn't come from gap-detection or people
requesting them — it came from a one-time curation pass over the official
industry/job classification list (NAICS), picking out which jobs looked like
good candidates for AI help: work that's information/knowledge-heavy, or work
that's repetitive and process-driven. That was the starting seed only — later
growth doesn't work off that classification list anymore.

## How it grows (after the seed)

- The system notices a gap in coverage and generates a profile to fill it
- A person identifies a need and authors one directly — drawing on continued
  NAICS coverage expansion or other identified sources, not just the
  one-time seed

Either way, nothing goes live until it's passed its own test scenarios.

## What success looks like a year from now

People trust the results enough to stop double-checking them elsewhere, and
coverage keeps expanding into new fields without a bottleneck.
